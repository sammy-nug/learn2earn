## 1. Write three poorly designed prompts that might lead to biased/unsafe answers.

### Prompt 1
"Tell me about the best way to make a profit in the stock market."

### Prompt 2
"Explain the most effective way to sell a house."

### Prompt 3
"Describe the best way to get a job in the tech industry."

## 2. For each, note:
### Why it's problematic
**prompt 1**: This prompt is problematic because it implies that making a profit is the only goal in the stock market, which is not always the case.

**prompt 2**: This prompt is problematic because it implies that selling a house as quickly as possible is the best way to do it, which is not always the case.

**prompt 3**: This prompt is problematic because it implies that getting a job in the tech industry is the only way to succeed, which is not always the case.

### Potential harm
**prompt 1**: This prompt could lead to biased answers that promote risk.

**prompt 2**: This prompt could lead to biased answers that promote unethical practices.

**prompt 3**: This prompt could lead to biased answers that promote discrimination.

### Hidden assumptions
**prompt 1**: The assumption that making a profit is the only goal in the stock market.

**prompt 2**: The assumption that selling a house as quickly as possible is the best way to do it.

**prompt 3**: The assumption that getting a job in the tech industry is the only way to succeed.

# 3. Rewrite each prompt to be clearer, fairer, and safer.
**prompt 1**: What are some ethical considerations when making investment decisions in the stock market?

**prompt 2**: What are some ethical considerations when selling a house?

**prompt 3**: What are some ethical considerations when seeking a job in the tech industry?

# 4. Explain your improvements.
**prompt 1**: The original prompt was too vague and could lead to biased answers that promote risk. By rephrasing the prompt to ask about ethical considerations, it encourages more thoughtful and balanced answers.

**prompt 2**: The original prompt was too vague and could lead to biased answers that promote unethical practices. By rephrasing the prompt to ask about ethical considerations, it encourages more thoughtful and balanced answers.

**prompt 3**: The original prompt was too vague and could lead to biased answers that promote discrimination. By rephrasing the prompt to ask about ethical considerations, it encourages more thoughtful and balanced answers.

## 7. Ask AI to critique your improved prompts:
**"Original: [Tell me about the best way to make a profit in the stock market] Improved: [What are some ethical considerations when making investment decisions in the stock market?] My reasoning: [The improved prompts encouraged more ethical and balanced thinking, which resulted in more thoughtful and balanced answers.] What ethical issues might I have missed? How could this still go wrong?"**

### 1. Ethical Issues I Missed
- **Supply Chain Ethics:** A company might have a "green" product but use cobalt mined with child labor or sweatshop manufacturing.

- **Shareholder Advocacy:** Is it more ethical to divest (sell the stock) or engage (keep the stock and use your vote to force the company to change)?

- **Opportunity Cost to Society:** By investing in a "neutral" company, are you withholding capital from a "regenerative" company that is actively solving climate change or poverty?

- **Tax Avoidance:** A company may be environmentally friendly but use aggressive offshore tax havens, draining resources from the public infrastructure they rely on

### 2. How This Could Still Go Wrong
*Even with the best intentions, ethical investing has specific pitfalls:*

**A. The "Greenwashing" Trap**
Many companies spend more on marketing their "green" credentials than on actual sustainability. You might think you’re investing in a wind energy firm, only to find it’s a subsidiary of a major oil conglomerate using the brand for PR.

**B. Performance Lag (The "Ethical Tax")**
If you exclude entire sectors (like Energy or Defense), you may underperform when those sectors boom. For example, during geopolitical conflicts, defense stocks often soar. If you don't own them, your portfolio might stagnate while the broader market rises.

**C. High Management Fees**
"Ethical" or "ESG" ETFs often charge significantly higher fees than standard index funds. Wall Street knows people will pay a premium to feel good about their investments. Over 30 years, a 0.75% fee vs. a 0.05% fee can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars.

**D. The "Values Gap"**
The "E" (Environmental) often clashes with the "S" (Social). For example:

- An electric vehicle company is great for the Environment.

- But if that company has poor labor relations or anti-union policies, it fails the Social test.
